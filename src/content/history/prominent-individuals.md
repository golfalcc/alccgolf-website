In the hallowed halls of Juniata Valley Colony Club and its transformation
into the thriving American Legion Country Club, the narrative is woven not
just by bricks and mortar but by the contributions of the individuals who
shaped its destiny.

## R.P.M. Davis — Architect of Dreams and Community Champion (1874–1964)

![R.P.M. Davis's Sta-Neet razor-and-comb invention](/images/people/davis-staneet.jpg)
*R.P.M. Davis's patented Sta-Neet razor-and-comb combination, one of his 200+ patents.*

![Honolulu Advertiser, September 28, 1948, covering the Sta-Neet](/images/archive/1948-davis-honolulu-advertiser.jpg)
*Davis's Sta-Neet invention made news as far away as the Honolulu Advertiser, September 28, 1948.*

Roy Paul Miller Davis, affectionately known as RPM, left a more profound
and lasting impact on the Juniata Valley Colony Club's history than almost
any other figure. The seeds of the golf course and community that became
the American Legion Country Club were sown in a Mount Union classroom in
1924, when Davis — a retired banker and manufacturer — convened the
meeting of local citizens and businessmen that set the project in motion.

A distinguished graduate of Princeton University (1898), Davis held a
presidency at Central National Bank, a superintendent role at
Harbison-Walker Refractories, and led North American Refractories as
builder, general manager, and director. He also held over 200 patents,
including the Sta-Neet razor-and-comb combination, which sold more than a
million units. Davis was known for employing handicapped workers,
including the visually impaired, on assembly lines at good wages — a
reflection of the same community-minded spirit he brought to founding the
club.

Davis's efforts drew prominent families to the Juniata Valley Colony Club,
envisioning it as a hidden mountain resort on the Walter Gifford farm. The
club that flourished from his idea stands today as a testament to his
foresight and commitment.

## Tom Bendelow — The Visionary Architect

![Portrait of Tom Bendelow](/images/people/bendelow.jpg)

Tom Bendelow, born in Aberdeen, Scotland in 1868, was one of the most
prolific American golf course architects of the early 20th century,
known as the "Johnny Appleseed of American Golf" for the estimated
600–700 courses he designed across the country during golf's first great
boom. Club history credits a letter from Bendelow to R.P.M. Davis with
envisioning the 18-hole course laid out on the rugged, picturesque terrain
of Mount Union. Bendelow's emphasis on simplicity and playability helped
make golf accessible to players of all skill levels, and his influence on
the sport's growth in America is still recognized today. He passed away in
1936.

Long before the American Legion Country Club became a centerpiece of
Mount Union, its clubhouse was shaped by one of Pennsylvania's most
accomplished and forward-thinking pioneers. Anna Wagner Keichline, a
Bellefonte native, began her studies at Penn State College before
transferring to Cornell University, where she earned her architecture
degree in 1911. In 1920, she became the first registered woman architect
in Pennsylvania.

Rather than pursuing large urban commissions, Keichline focused on the
buildings that shape everyday life — homes, schools, churches, theaters,
and community gathering spaces across central Pennsylvania — designed with
an emphasis on efficiency, durability, and thoughtful use of space. She
was also a prolific inventor, patenting the "K Brick," a hollow, fireproof
clay building unit decades ahead of its time and a forerunner of the
modern concrete block.

Between 1927 and 1928, Keichline designed the original clubhouse at what
is now the American Legion Country Club — a structure built for community
and connection that still serves as the club's central hub nearly a
century later. Much of what is known about her today is thanks to the
research and preservation work of her great-niece, Nancy J. Perkins.
